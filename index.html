Global Governance

Definition

Global governance is the system of institutions, agreements, and processes through which countries (and other actors) manage problems that affect the whole world. It does not mean a "world government." It means cooperation across borders to deal with shared issues like conflict, climate change, trade, pandemics, and human rights.

States vs. Countries (and why the UN often says "states")

Country (common meaning)

A place or nation that people recognize as a distinct area with its own identity (culture, population, territory).

Example: "The Philippines is a country."

State (international law meaning)

A political entity that has a government and is recognized as having authority over a territory and population, and can make official relations with other states (like signing treaties).

Example: "The Philippines is a UN member state and can sign treaties."

Simple way to remember:

  • "Country" is the common word.
  • "State" is the legal/political word used in diplomacy.

Example where the difference matters:

A territory might be called a "country" by some people informally, but may not be a sovereign state recognized widely enough to join the UN as a member state.

In the UN, membership is for states, because the UN is built on legal recognition and official diplomatic responsibilities (treaty-making, voting, obligations).

How global governance works in real life

Global governance operates through a mix of formal and informal cooperation:

1

Formal institutions

Example: the United Nations (UN) holds meetings, passes decisions, organizes peacekeeping, supports humanitarian work.

2

Treaties (international agreements)

Example: countries sign treaties to limit nuclear weapons or reduce carbon emissions.

3

Smaller global groups

Example: the G20 gathers major economies to coordinate on global finance and economic stability.

4

Non-government actors (NGOs and companies)

NGOs (nonprofit groups) and businesses influence global decisions through advocacy, expertise, funding, and implementation.

Example: NGOs help deliver humanitarian aid during crises; companies may shape global supply chains and climate commitments.

2

Treaties

Definition

A treaty is a formal, written agreement between countries (states). In international law, a treaty is generally legally binding once a country formally accepts it (often through a process called ratification, depending on the country's legal system).

Purpose of treaties

Treaties are used to:

  • Set shared rules (what countries must do or must not do)
  • Reduce conflict
  • Solve global problems (environment, weapons, trade, human rights)

Examples

CLIMATE

Paris Agreement

Countries commit to actions that reduce greenhouse gas emissions to address climate change.

NUCLEAR

Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty (NPT)

Limits the spread of nuclear weapons, supports disarmament goals, and allows peaceful nuclear energy under monitoring.

3

Proliferation and the Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty (NPT)

Definition of proliferation

Proliferation means the spread or increase of something.

Nuclear proliferation means more countries gaining or developing nuclear weapons.

What the NPT is

The Treaty on the Non-Proliferation of Nuclear Weapons (NPT) is a major global treaty designed to reduce the risk of nuclear war by limiting the spread of nuclear weapons and encouraging safer nuclear cooperation.

What happens under the NPT (the "3 pillars")

1

Non-proliferation

  • • Countries without nuclear weapons agree not to acquire or build them.
  • • Countries with nuclear weapons agree not to help others get them.
2

Disarmament

Nuclear-armed states commit to working toward reducing nuclear weapons over time.

3

Peaceful use of nuclear energy

Countries can use nuclear technology for peaceful purposes (like electricity), with safeguards/monitoring.

Real-life example of function

The treaty supports international inspection and monitoring systems to reduce secret nuclear weapons development, while still allowing peaceful nuclear programs.

4

United Nations (UN)

What the UN is

The United Nations is a global organization founded in 1945 after World War II. Its purpose is to prevent future large-scale wars, promote international cooperation, and address global problems like poverty, disease, conflict, and human rights abuses.

Major events that led to its creation

World War II (1939–1945)

Massive destruction convinced many leaders that a stronger international organization was needed to prevent another world war.

Earlier failure of the League of Nations

Showed what happens when an international organization lacks power and participation.

UN Charter (1945)

Countries met to create the founding document that set the UN's goals, principles, and structure.

5

League of Nations (Background)

What it was

The League of Nations was created after World War I to maintain peace through cooperation and diplomacy.

Why it failed

  • • Key powers did not participate fully (notably, the U.S. never joined).
  • • It lacked strong enforcement power.
  • • When major aggressor states ignored it, the League could not stop them.
  • • It failed to prevent World War II.

Six Main Organs of the United Nations

1

General Assembly (GA)

Definition & composition

The main "discussion and decision forum" of the UN. All 193 member states are represented. Each member has one vote.

Core distinction:

Most inclusive UN organ: every member state participates equally.

Functions

  • Discusses major global issues (peace, development, human rights).
  • Approves the UN budget.
  • Elects members to other UN bodies (including non-permanent Security Council members).
  • Passes resolutions (statements/decisions).

Non-binding resolutions (meaning)

A non-binding resolution is not legally enforceable. Countries are not legally required to follow it the way they must follow a treaty or a binding Security Council decision.

However, non-binding resolutions can still matter because they:

  • show global opinion or international pressure
  • guide future treaties and national policies
  • influence what is considered normal/acceptable behavior internationally

Real-life examples:

  • • Adoption of the Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948) (not a treaty, but highly influential).
  • • Adoption of major global goals and frameworks that guide development priorities worldwide.
2

Security Council (UNSC)

Definition & composition

The organ responsible for maintaining international peace and security.

5
Permanent members (P5)
US, UK, France, China, Russia
10
Non-permanent members
Rotating seats

Core distinction:

Strongest UN organ for peace/security decisions: can issue binding decisions, authorize sanctions, and authorize military or peacekeeping action.

Veto power (meaning)

The veto is a special power held by the 5 permanent members.

If any one of them votes "no" on a major (substantive) resolution, it can block the resolution even if many others support it.

How the 10 non-permanent (rotating) members are chosen and voted

1

What "rotating" means:

The 10 seats are not permanent; different countries take turns serving. Each serves a 2-year term. Elections are designed to ensure geographic representation (so regions of the world have a chance to be represented).

2

How they are elected (voted):

They are elected by the General Assembly. Voting is done by UN member states in the General Assembly. A country must typically win a two-thirds majority of the General Assembly members present and voting to get elected.

Seats are allocated by region:

Africa Asia-Pacific Latin America and the Caribbean Western Europe and Others Eastern Europe

Real-life examples of function:

  • • Authorizing peacekeeping missions in conflict areas.
  • • Imposing sanctions on groups or governments considered threats to peace.
3

Economic and Social Council (ECOSOC)

Definition & composition

Composed of 54 member states elected by the General Assembly (terms are typically 3 years).

Core distinction:

Focuses on economic and social development and coordination across UN agencies.

Functions

  • Coordinates work on poverty, education, health, jobs, inequality.
  • Works with UN specialized agencies and NGOs.
  • Helps follow up and coordinate global development plans.

Real-life example:

Organizes the High-Level Political Forum (HLPF) where countries review progress on the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs).

4

International Court of Justice (ICJ)

Definition & composition

The main judicial organ of the UN. Located in The Hague, Netherlands. Has 15 judges elected for 9-year terms by the General Assembly and Security Council.

Core distinction:

Handles legal disputes between states (not individuals).

Functions

  • Settles disputes about borders, treaties, sovereignty, and international law.
  • Gives advisory opinions when asked by UN bodies.

Real-life example:

The ICJ decided the Nigeria–Cameroon territorial case involving the Bakassi Peninsula.

5

Secretariat

Definition & composition

The administrative and operational arm of the UN. Led by the Secretary-General and staffed by international civil servants worldwide.

Core distinction:

Implements and runs UN work day-to-day (it does not vote like the GA or UNSC).

Functions

  • Organizes UN meetings and conferences.
  • Produces reports and research.
  • Helps manage peacekeeping, humanitarian response, and UN programs.

Real-life example:

Supports major UN conferences and ongoing coordination for climate and humanitarian initiatives.

6

Trusteeship Council

Definition & composition

Created to supervise trust territories (former colonies/territories placed under UN oversight) to guide them toward self-government or independence.

Core distinction:

It had a specific historical mission (decolonization) and is now largely inactive.

Functions (historical)

  • Monitored progress of trust territories.
  • Reviewed reports and conducted missions.
  • Encouraged steps toward independence.

Last trust territory (meaning and example)

The "last trust territory" means the final territory still under UN trusteeship supervision.

Palau was the last trust territory; it became independent in 1994.

After Palau's independence, the Trusteeship Council suspended regular operations.
